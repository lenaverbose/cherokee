The headline

In its first five months, the email & SMS program generated $1.07M in attributed revenue. The story underneath it is concentration: automated flows produce nearly three-quarters of that revenue from under 10% of all sends, while broadcast campaigns do the heavy lifting on volume. By channel, email drives ~$873K (82%) and SMS ~$197K (18%) — SMS is small in volume but the most efficient dollar in the program. The gap to close is what happens after the open: clicks and conversion.

The retention headline: ~75% of buyers are one-and-done.

Order values are healthy and stable (first order ~$88, blended AOV ~$114), but only ~25% of customers place a second order, and the typical repeat buyer waits ~6–9 weeks. Converting more first-time buyers into a second purchase is the single biggest profit lever — a natural target for post-purchase / replenishment flows (see Post Purchase in Flows). A small set of bulk orders (~5%) carries an outsized ~25% of revenue, so protecting those accounts matters too.

Key takeaways

✓
Flows are the engine — and they're durable.

Flows delivered $790K (74%) of program revenue while costing under 10% of send volume. Welcome (email Welcome Series + the separate SMS Welcome flow) together total $560K — 52% of all program revenue. This is recurring, low-cost, always-on revenue that scales with traffic and list growth, not with send effort.

!
Campaigns are an under-monetized lever.

Campaigns carry 90% of sends but only 26% of revenue at $0.04 per send. Industry campaigns average closer to ~$0.11 RPR. The bottleneck is click-through (email 0.53% vs. a 2–5% norm), not deliverability or opens — closing even part of that gap is a 6–7 figure opportunity.

▲
High opens, low clicks — the conversion gap.

Email campaign open rate is 52% (well above the ~38% norm) but only ~1% of opens turn into clicks. Subject lines are winning attention; creative, offers and CTAs aren't converting it. This is the program's biggest controllable upside.

What the tests tell us

▲
Nothing is statistically significant — the tests are underpowered.

All four tests have p-values of 0.75–1.0 on conversion, with only 8–36 orders per variation. At that volume you can't reliably separate a real winner from chance. The revenue-per-recipient gaps (even the eye-catching +51%) are driven by a handful of differently-sized orders, not a true conversion difference.

▲
The "winner" flips depending on the metric.

In three of four tests, the variation that won on revenue-per-recipient lost on conversion rate (and vice versa). Example: the live Browse Abandonment creative test shows the image headline up +51% on RPR, but the text headline actually converted slightly more people. Pick one primary metric before a test starts.

!
Build a real testing cadence.

Only one genuinely new test launched in 2026 (the January send-time test, which tied). The flow experiments date back to April 2025 and are still running. Run fewer, bigger, longer tests — set a primary metric, a minimum sample/order threshold, and let Klaviyo's built-in significance call the winner before you ship it.

!
Clean up the duplicate Browse Abandonment test.

The same image-vs-text experiment is running under both a live message and a leftover "Copy of" draft — and they report opposite directional winners. Consolidate to a single live version so the data isn't split and contradictory.

Lead & purchase cohorts

The two cohort lenses you asked for. Lead cohorts group people by the month they first joined an email/SMS list — the closest proxy we have to a "lead" until a lead-form event exists — and track how many go on to a first order. Purchase cohorts group people by their first order and track repeat value over time. Together they answer: as paid scales with New Engine, is each new monthly intake converting better or worse, and what is a customer worth over time? Focus is Jan 2026 forward (marked ●); 2025 cohorts are kept only as a maturity benchmark, since they're old enough to show the full-year curve.

What the cohorts tell us

✓
2026 lead quality is trending up, not diluting.

Month-0 lead→order conversion climbed steadily through 2026 — Jan 19.0% → Feb 19.5% → Mar 20.7% → Apr 22.2% → May 26.7%, the best of any cohort on record. As the New Engine paid program ramps and intake grows, newer cohorts are converting faster, not worse — an encouraging early read on acquisition quality and CAC efficiency.

⚠︎
Dec 2025 is the cautionary cohort.

Only 9.3% of December subscribers placed a first order in month 0 — less than half the ~20% norm — and the cohort never caught up (~15% cumulative through 6 months vs. ~28–32% for Jun/Jul). That's the signature of a holiday/promo-driven influx of low-intent, deal-seeking subscribers. As paid scales, watch month-0 conversion by source/cohort as the early-warning metric — volume isn't the same as quality.

▲
Lead value is decided fast — judge cohorts in weeks, not quarters.

Across every cohort, the bulk of first-year purchase activity happens in month 0; by month 1 the rate falls to ~2–4%, and from month 3 on it's a thin ~0.6–1%/month tail. That's the answer to "why higher in month 1 than month 3" — the curve front-loads hard. Practically: a cohort's month-0 conversion all but tells you where it will land, so you can read a new paid source's quality within ~30 days rather than waiting a quarter.

▲
Repeat orders add a third to half of customer value.

First orders average ~$109; mature 2025 buyer cohorts go on to spend another 33–43% over the following year (Jun '25 buyers: $122 first order → ~$175 cumulative per buyer). The 2026 cohorts are tracking the same early shape. This is the LTV side of the CAC:LTV ratio New Engine needs to underwrite paid spend.

!
Post Purchase is the lever on the repeat tail.

Repeat revenue is heavily front-loaded into months 0–1, then thins out. The clearest unlock is the matching-set / coordinating-item push in the Post Purchase flow (see Flows tab) — turning one-item buyers into repeat buyers is what bends the purchase-cohort curve upward and compounds the cumulative-value lines above.

Prioritized action plan

Ranked by estimated revenue impact vs. effort. The two biggest levers — fixing the conversion gap on campaigns and closing the abandoned-cart benchmark gap — are also the most controllable.

The 90-day shortlist

1
Fix the Abandoned Cart flow to industry RPR.

Closing the email leg from $0.82 toward $1.71 (what the Cherokee flow already achieves) is a direct 6-figure annualized lift. Audit send timing, rewrite message 1, promote winning "Copy of"/"Repeat" variants, and expand the higher-converting SMS leg.

2
Build out Post Purchase to grow LTV.

When a customer buys a single piece that belongs to a matching set, follow up by recommending the coordinating item. It turns a one-item buyer into a repeat purchaser and lifts lifetime value — on an audience that already opens at 41–43%.

3
Rebuild Welcome emails 2 & 3.

Email 1 converts at 4.0% but emails 2 & 3 collapse to 0.2% and together earn only 7% of a $419K flow. Give them a real reason to click — a fresh hook, a category nudge, or a soft incentive.

4
Scale what already wins: SMS & seasonal.

Grow SMS list capture (SMS RPR > email in welcome, cart and most campaign themes) and build the campaign calendar around Nurses-Week-style identity moments, which out-earn standard sales.
